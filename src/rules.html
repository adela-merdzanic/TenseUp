Grammar rules

Tenses at a glance

Most tense questions are decided by a signal word somewhere in the sentence. Find it first, then pick the tense.

Tense Use it for Signal words Example
Present Simple habits, routines, facts, timetables always, usually, often, every day, never The server backs up data every night.
Present Continuous happening now; temporary or changing situations now, at the moment, currently, Look!, these days The system is updating right now.
Past Simple finished action at a known past time yesterday, ago, last week, in 1984 Apple released the first Mac in 1984.
Past Continuous action in progress at a past moment; background to a shorter action while, as, at 10 p.m. yesterday I was installing Linux when the power went out.
Present Perfect past action with a result now; experience; unfinished time period just, already, yet, ever, never, since, for, so far I have just finished the report.
Present Perfect Continuous emphasises how long something has been going on (and still is) for, since, all morning, how long She has been working here since 2020.
Past Perfect the earlier of two past actions before, after, by the time, when The download had finished before I got back.

Picking the right past tense

  • Long background action interrupted by a short one → past continuous + past simple: I was waiting for the download when the connection dropped.
  • Two actions one after the other → both past simple: I opened the file and fixed the bug.
  • One action clearly before the other → the earlier one takes past perfect: When I arrived, the meeting had already started.
State verbs (know, want, believe, like, need, own, understand) are almost never used in continuous forms: I know the answer, not I am knowing.
since vs for: since + starting point (since Monday, since 2020); for + duration (for two hours, for three years).

Future forms: will, going to, present continuous

Form Use it for Example
will + infinitive decision made at the moment of speaking; predictions and opinions (I think…); promises and offers The phone is ringing - I'll answer it.
going to + infinitive plan or intention decided before speaking; prediction based on present evidence We're going to upgrade the network next month. Look at those clouds - it's going to rain.
Present Continuous fixed arrangement, usually with a time and place I'm meeting the professor on Tuesday at 10.
Present Simple timetables and schedules The exam starts at 9:00.
Rule of thumb: decided now → will; decided earlier → going to; already arranged with someone → present continuous.

Conditionals

Type If-clause Main clause Use it for Example
Zero present simple present simple facts, things that are always true If you delete a file, it goes to the recycle bin.
First present simple will + infinitive real, likely future situations If the test passes, we will deploy the update.
Second past simple would + infinitive unreal or unlikely present/future If I had more RAM, the build would run faster.
Third past perfect would have + past participle unreal past - it didn't happen; regrets If you had made a backup, you wouldn't have lost the data.
  • Never put will or would in the if-clause. If you go out…, not if you will go.
  • In the second conditional, use were for all persons: If I were you…
  • When the if-clause comes first, put a comma after it: If it rains, we'll stay in.

unless, as long as, providing

  • unless = if … not: Unless you save the file, you'll lose your changes = If you don't save… (don't add another not after unless).
  • as long as / provided (that) / providing (that) = only if - they follow the first-conditional pattern: You can use my laptop as long as you are careful.

Participle clauses

Participles shorten relative clauses (which/that…) in definitions - very common in technical English.

  • -ing (present participle) = active meaning: a device connecting two networks (= a device which connects…).
  • -ed / third form (past participle) = passive meaning: data stored in the cloud (= data which is stored…).

Ask: does the noun do the action (→ -ing) or is the action done to it (→ -ed)?

Typical exam transformation: A bridge is a device which links two networks → A bridge is a device linking two networks. And passive: a program which is used for browsing → a program used for browsing.

Both / either / neither

Word Meaning Pattern Example
both the one AND the other (2 of 2) both … and - plural verb Both Windows and Linux are operating systems.
either the one OR the other (1 of 2) either … or You can use either Chrome or Firefox.
neither NOT the one and NOT the other (0 of 2) neither … nor - verb stays positive Neither the mouse nor the keyboard works.

When do you need "of"?

  • Directly before a noun: no of - both computers, either option, neither answer.
  • Before the / these / my + noun: of is optional with both (both (of) the students) but required with either/neither (either of the options, neither of the servers).
  • Before a pronoun: of is required - both of them, either of us, neither of them.

Active & passive

Passive = be (in the tense you need) + past participle. Use it when the doer is unknown, obvious, or unimportant; add by + agent only if it matters.

Tense Passive form Example
Present Simple is/are + stored Backups are stored on the server.
Present Continuous is/are being + stored The update is being installed now.
Past Simple was/were + stored Linux was created by Linus Torvalds.
Present Perfect has/have been + stored The bug has been fixed.
will will be + stored The results will be published tomorrow.
Modals (can, must…) can be + stored The file can be downloaded for free.
Deciding active vs passive in a gap: find the subject and ask - does it do the action, or is the action done to it? The email was sent yesterday (someone sent it), but She sent the email yesterday.

mustn't vs don't have to

They look similar but mean opposite things:

  • mustn't = it is prohibited, don't do it: You mustn't share your password.
  • don't have to = it is not necessary, but you can if you want: You don't have to pay - the software is free.

For obligation, must and have to are close in meaning: must often expresses the speaker's own rule, have to an external rule (I have to wear a badge at work).

Prepositions: at, in, on (and to)

Time

Preposition Used with Examples
at clock times; fixed phrases at 9 o'clock, at noon, at night, at the weekend, at Christmas
in months, years, seasons, centuries; parts of the day; "after X time" in July, in 2026, in winter, in the morning, in ten minutes
on days and dates; day + part of day on Monday, on 4 May, on my birthday, on Friday morning

Place

Preposition Used for Examples
at a point or a place seen as a point at the door, at work, at school, at the bus stop
in inside something; towns, countries in the office, in a folder, in Mostar, in Bosnia
on a surface on the desk, on the screen, on the wall, on the internet
to movement, direction go to work, travel to Sarajevo, upload it to the server
Watch out: at night but in the evening; arrive in a city but arrive at a building; at home and go home (no to); no preposition before next / last / this / every (next Monday, not on next Monday).

make / do & take / put

make vs do

  • do - work-like activities and general tasks: do homework, do a job, do business, do research, do the shopping, do your best, do someone a favour.
  • make - creating or producing something, plus many fixed phrases: make a decision, make a mistake, make progress, make money, make a phone call, make an appointment, make an effort, make sure.

take vs put

  • take: take part in (participate), take place (happen), take care of, take over (assume control), take up (start a hobby / occupy space), take advantage of, take notes, take your time.
  • put: put off (postpone), put up with (tolerate), put forward (suggest), put through (connect a call), put on, put away.
Collocations can't be worked out from logic - they're fixed pairs. When you get one wrong in practice, learn the whole phrase, not just the verb.

Making questions

  • Word order: (Question word) + auxiliary + subject + main verb - What does LAN stand for?
  • Present and past simple need do / does / did, and the main verb goes back to its base form: Did you install it?, not Did you installed.
  • If who or what is the subject of the question, there is no auxiliary: Who invented the World Wide Web?
  • With be and with other auxiliaries (have, will, can…), just invert: Is the network down? Have you finished?

The prefixes e- and cyber-

  • e- = electronic, done over the internet: e-mail, e-commerce, e-learning, e-book, e-banking.
  • cyber- = connected with computers and the internet (often security-related): cybersecurity, cybercrime, cyberattack, cyberspace, cyberbullying.

Irregular verbs

Base form → past simple → past participle. The past simple is used on its own (I wrote the report yesterday); the past participle appears in the present/past perfect, the passive, and the third conditional (I have written, it was written, you would have written).

Exam tips

  • Read the whole sentence before choosing - the signal word that decides the tense is often at the end.
  • In key-word transformations: keep the meaning, don't change the given word, and check the word count if one is required.
  • For if-sentences, identify the type first (real future? unreal present? unreal past?) - then the verb forms follow automatically.
  • In this app, multiple-answer questions require you to select every correct option - a partial answer counts as wrong.
